## The Three Tiers of Business Websites

### Tier 1: Template-Based Website — $500 to $1,500

This is your entry-level option. Someone takes a pre-built template from WordPress, Squarespace, or Wix, customizes it with your branding and content, and hands it over.

**What you get:**
- 3-5 pages (Home, About, Services, Contact, maybe a Gallery)
- A pre-designed layout with your logo, colors, and text swapped in
- Basic contact form
- Mobile-responsive design (most modern templates are)
- Basic SEO setup (page titles, meta descriptions)

**What you don't get:**
- Custom design tailored to your brand
- Unique functionality or features
- Optimized performance (templates are often bloated)
- Ongoing support or updates (usually)
- A site that stands out from competitors using the same template

**Who this is right for:** Very early-stage businesses that need *something* online right now and have a tight budget. A contractor who just needs a digital business card. A new restaurant that mainly relies on foot traffic but wants a basic web presence.

**The catch:** Template sites can look generic, and they often come with hidden costs. That $12/month Squarespace plan adds up to $144/year, and you're locked into their platform. WordPress templates need plugin updates, security patches, and hosting — neglect these and you'll end up with a hacked or broken site within a year or two.

### Tier 2: Custom-Designed Website — $1,500 to $5,000

This is the sweet spot for most small businesses. A developer designs and builds your site from scratch (or heavily customizes a starting point), tailored specifically to your business.

**What you get:**
- 5-15 pages with custom design
- Unique layout and visual identity that matches your brand
- Performance optimization (fast load times)
- Proper SEO foundation with local keyword targeting
- Mobile-first design
- Contact forms, lead capture, maybe a blog
- Integration with tools you already use (Google Analytics, email marketing, booking systems)
- Responsive support during and after the build

**What you don't get:**
- Complex web application features (user accounts, dashboards, e-commerce with hundreds of products)
- Ongoing content creation (though some developers offer this as an add-on)

**Who this is right for:** Established small businesses that want to actually compete online. Service businesses (landscapers, electricians, attorneys, medical practices), restaurants, charter companies, real estate agents — basically anyone whose website is a primary way customers find and evaluate them.

**Why this tier wins:** At this price point, you're getting a website that actually reflects your business, ranks on Google, and converts visitors into customers. The ROI on going from a bad website (or no website) to a well-built custom site is usually measurable within the first few months.

### Tier 3: Custom Web Application — $3,000 to $10,000+

This is for businesses that need their website to *do* something beyond informing and converting. We're talking about actual software functionality built into the site.

**What you get:**
- Everything in Tier 2, plus:
- Custom booking/scheduling systems
- Customer portals or dashboards
- E-commerce with inventory management
- Database-driven features (member directories, property listings, course catalogs)
- API integrations with third-party services
- Admin panels for managing content and data
- User authentication and accounts

**Who this is right for:** Businesses where the website IS the product or a core operational tool. A charter company that needs real-time booking and payment processing. A property management firm that needs a tenant portal. An e-commerce business with complex product configurations. A membership organization with gated content.

**The ceiling is high here.** Complex web apps with lots of custom features can easily run $15,000-$50,000+ at agencies. The $3,000-$10,000 range is achievable when you're working with an efficient developer who uses modern tools and frameworks (like Next.js, which I use) rather than a team of 12 billing by the hour.

## What Actually Affects the Price

Understanding the tiers is step one. Here's what pushes the price up or down within each tier:

### Number of Pages

More pages = more design, more content, more development time. A 5-page site is straightforward. A 20-page site with multiple service pages, a blog, a gallery, a team page, and location pages is significantly more work.

### Custom Design vs. Template Customization

Starting from a blank canvas costs more than modifying an existing template. But the result is a site that's uniquely yours and optimized for your specific goals.

### Features and Functionality

Every feature adds development time:
- **Contact form:** Simple, usually included in base price
- **Blog:** Moderate addition
- **Photo gallery:** Moderate
- **Online booking:** Significant addition
- **E-commerce:** Significant to major, depending on complexity
- **Customer portal:** Major addition
- **Custom integrations:** Varies wildly

### Content Creation

Do you have professional photos, written copy, and your branding ready to go? Or does the developer need to help with copywriting, source stock photos, and figure out your brand guidelines? Content creation can add $500-$2,000+ to a project.

### Ongoing Maintenance

Websites aren't "set and forget." They need hosting, security updates, backups, content updates, and occasional fixes. Budget for:
- **Hosting:** $10-$50/month for most business sites
- **Domain:** $10-$15/year
- **SSL certificate:** Usually free with modern hosting
- **Maintenance:** $50-$200/month if you want someone handling updates and monitoring

## Why "Cheap" Costs More in the Long Run

I talk to business owners all the time who paid $300 for a website three years ago and now need to start over from scratch. Here's how that math usually works out:

| Approach | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Total |
|----------|--------|--------|--------|-------|
| **Cheap site** ($300, then rebuild) | $300 | $0 (site declining) | $2,500 (rebuild) | $2,800 |
| **Quality site** ($2,500, then maintain) | $2,500 | $600 (maintenance) | $600 (maintenance) | $3,700 |

Looks like the cheap route saves money, right? But factor in the **revenue lost** during those two years when your cheap site was underperforming — slow load times, poor mobile experience, bad SEO, low conversion rates. If a better website would have brought in even 2-3 extra customers per month at $200 average value, that's:

**3 extra customers x $200 x 24 months = $14,400 in lost revenue**

The "cheap" option that saved you $900 actually cost you $14,400. This is the trap most small businesses fall into.

## What About DIY?

Squarespace and Wix make it look easy. And for some people, it works fine. But here's what I see in practice:

**The time cost is real.** Most business owners spend 40-80 hours trying to build their own site. At whatever you value your time at, that's a significant investment — and you'll still end up with something that looks like a template.

**The results show.** DIY sites almost always have slower performance, worse SEO, and lower conversion rates than professionally built sites. The platforms are great for ease-of-use, but they make trade-offs on speed and flexibility that hurt you long-term.

**The ongoing headaches.** When something breaks, when you need to add a feature, when Google changes its algorithm — you're on your own. Or you're paying someone to fix a site they didn't build, which always costs more.

DIY makes sense if you genuinely enjoy building websites and have the time. For most business owners, it's a false economy.

## Questions to Ask Any Web Developer Before Hiring

Whether you work with me or someone else, ask these questions:

1. **Can I see live sites you've built?** Not mockups — actual working websites.
2. **What platform/framework do you use?** Make sure you understand the long-term implications.
3. **What's included in the price?** Get a detailed scope in writing.
4. **Who owns the code/site when it's done?** You should own everything.
5. **What are the ongoing costs?** Hosting, domain, maintenance — get the full picture.
6. **What's the timeline?** And what happens if it goes over?
7. **Do you handle SEO?** At least a basic SEO foundation should be included.
8. **What happens after launch?** Is there a support period? What does maintenance cost?
